PA· KU· MU

The three Swahili locatives — how to talk about WHERE something is, happens, or goes.

PA Exact place 📍
KU General / movement 🗺️
MU Inside / within 📦

🌍 What are PA, KU, MU?

In English we use just one word for location — "at", "in", "to". In Swahili, there are three different locative markers depending on HOW you're describing the location.

Think of it this way:

PA = you can point your finger at exactly where it is. "Right there!"
KU = a general area, direction, or movement toward a place. "Over there somewhere / going to..."
MU = something is INSIDE a space or container. "In there / within..."

PA — definite spot · KU — direction / general · MU — inside / enclosed
PA
PA — The Exact Spot
Use PA when you're talking about a specific, definite location — like pointing at something with your finger. "Right here. Right there." You know exactly where it is.
= AT · ON · NEAR (specific)
📍

Think of it as a PIN on a map

When you drop a pin on Google Maps — that's PA. A precise, known, fixed point. "The book is right there on the table." You're not guessing — you can see it!

✅
A specific named location Somewhere you know by name — a city, a building, a specific room
✅
Near / next to something Describing proximity — "near the door", "next to the tree"
✅
On a surface Something sitting ON top of something else
✅
Answering "where exactly?" When someone wants to know the precise spot
Examples — PA in action
Kitabu kipo mezani.
The book is on the table.
ki-po = it is (PA form) | mezani = on the table
Nipo hapa.
I am right here.
ni-po = I am (PA) | hapa = here (exact)
Yeye yuko mlangoni.
He/She is at the door.
yu-ko = he/she is (PA) | mlangoni = at the door
Wanaishi Nairobi.
They live in Nairobi.
PA — Nairobi is a specific, known place
Kaa pale.
Sit right there.
pale = right there (specific visible spot)
Duka lipo pembeni.
The shop is nearby.
li-po = it is (PA) | pembeni = nearby, next to
KU
KU — Direction & General Place
Use KU for movement toward a place, a general area (not pinpointed), or when the location is vague / unspecified. Also used with infinitive verbs (to go, to run, etc.).
= TO · TOWARDS · GENERALLY AT
🗺️

Think of it as a region on a map — no pin, just an area

You say "I'm going towards town" — you're not at a specific spot, you're heading in a direction. That's KU. It's also the prefix for ALL Swahili infinitives — ku-la (to eat), ku-enda (to go), ku-imba (to sing).

✅
Movement / going TO a place "I am going to school" — direction of travel
✅
A general, unspecified area "Somewhere over there" — you don't know exactly where
✅
Infinitive verbs (to + verb) Every Swahili verb in its base form starts with KU — ku-la, ku-imba, ku-soma
✅
Far away / over there (general) When something is distant but not precisely pointed at
Examples — KU in action
Ninaenda shuleni.
I am going to school.
Going TO = KU direction | shuleni = to school
Yuko mbali.
He/She is far away.
yu-ko = he/she is (KU form) | mbali = far
Ninataka kwenda sokoni.
I want to go to the market.
kwenda = ku + enda (to go) — KU infinitive
Watoto wako nje.
The children are outside.
wa-ko = they are (KU) | nje = outside (general)
Nenda huko.
Go over there.
huko = over there (general, KU direction)
Anapenda kusoma.
He/She likes to read.
ku-soma = to read — KU infinitive form
MU
MU — Inside & Within
Use MU when something is inside, within, or enclosed by something — a building, a container, a room, a country, a body of water. Anything with an interior that you can be inside of.
= IN · INSIDE · WITHIN
📦

Think of it as being INSIDE a box

If you can physically be inside something — a room, a building, a country, a bag, a river — that's MU. The key question is: "Is there a surrounding space you're enclosed within?" If yes, use MU.

✅
Inside a room or building "In the house", "In the classroom", "In the hospital"
✅
Inside a container or object "In the bag", "In the box", "In the cup"
✅
Inside a country or large area "In Kenya", "In the forest", "In the water"
✅
Within a group or abstract space "In the family", "In my heart", "In my mind"
Examples — MU in action
Niko nyumbani.
I am in the house.
ni-ko = I am (MU form here) | nyumbani = in the house
Kitabu kimo mfukoni.
The book is in the bag.
ki-mo = it is (MU form) | mfukoni = in the bag
Wanaishi Kenya.
They live in Kenya.
MU — inside the country of Kenya
Samaki wamo majini.
The fish are in the water.
wa-mo = they are (MU) | majini = in the water
Daktari yuko hospitalini.
The doctor is in the hospital.
MU — inside the building
Moyo wangu uko nawe.
My heart is with you.
Abstract — within the space of being with you

⚡ The "To Be" Verb — PA · KU · MU Forms

This is the most important part. The verb kuwa (to be) changes its subject prefix suffix depending on which locative you use:

PA
I amNipo
You areUpo
He/She isYupo
We areTupo
They areWapo
KU
I amNiko
You areUko
He/She isYuko
We areTuko
They areWako
MU
I amNimo
You areUmo
He/She isYumo
We areTumo
They areWamo
🔑 Memory trick for the endings:
PA → ends in –PO (think: "spot-PO" — a specific spot!)
KU → ends in –KO (think: "go-KO" — going somewhere!)
MU → ends in –MO (think: "roOM-O" — inside a room!)

🏠 Same Place — Three Different Meanings

Look at how PA, KU and MU give three completely different meanings even with the same place word nyumba (house):

PA
Yupo nyumbani.
He/She is at the house. (right there, at that specific house)
yu-PO = he/she is (PA) — exact known location
KU
Yuko nyumbani.
He/She is home. (generally at home, somewhere around the house)
yu-KO = he/she is (KU) — general location / direction
MU
Yumo nyumbani.
He/She is inside the house. (physically inside the building)
yu-MO = he/she is (MU) — inside the enclosed space
💬 Real talk: In everyday spoken Swahili, –ko (KU) is the most commonly used form and often substitutes for all three when the meaning is obvious from context. You'll hear "Yuko nyumbani" for all situations. But knowing all three makes your Swahili precise and impressive! 🌟

🔤 KU — Also the Infinitive Prefix

Here's where KU gets really interesting. Every single Swahili verb in its base form starts with KU–. This is exactly the same KU — it marks the action as something general, not tied to any specific person or time yet. Think of it as "to do something" — open-ended, no direction, no subject. Just the idea of the action.

The Pattern: KU– + verb stem = "to [verb]"
kusoma
to read / to study
ku + soma
kula
to eat
ku + la
kuimba
to sing
ku + imba
kuenda
to go
ku + enda
kusema
to speak / to say
ku + sema
kulala
to sleep
ku + lala
kupenda
to love / to like
ku + penda
kunywa
to drink
ku + nywa
kucheza
to play
ku + cheza
kufanya
to do / to make
ku + fanya
kukaa
to sit / to stay
ku + kaa
kuja
to come
ku + ja
🔑 The connection between KU (locative) and KU (infinitive):
They are the SAME KU! When you say "ninaenda shuleni" (I am going to school) the direction is KU — general, open-ended, heading toward something. When you say "kusoma" (to read) — it's also open-ended, not yet tied to who or when. Both express something general and not pinned down. That's the heart of KU.
KU infinitive used in real sentences
→
Ninapenda kusoma.
I like to read.
ninapenda = I like | ku-soma = to read (infinitive)
→
Anataka kula.
He/She wants to eat.
anataka = he/she wants | ku-la = to eat (infinitive)
→
Ni vizuri kulala mapema.
It is good to sleep early.
ni vizuri = it is good | ku-lala = to sleep | mapema = early
→
Ninajua kusema Kiswahili!
I know how to speak Swahili!
ninajua = I know | ku-sema = to speak | Kiswahili = Swahili
→
Kuimba ni furaha.
Singing is joy. / To sing is joyful.
ku-imba = to sing (subject of the sentence!) | furaha = joy
⚠️ What happens when you conjugate a verb?
When you actually USE the verb in a sentence with a subject and tense, the KU– drops off and gets replaced by the subject prefix + tense marker.

ku-soma (to read) → Ni-na-soma (I am reading)
ku-la (to eat) → A-li-kula (He/She ate)
ku-imba (to sing) → Tu-ta-imba (We will sing)

The KU is like the verb's "resting state." Once it wakes up and gets a subject, KU steps aside!
