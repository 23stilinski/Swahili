How Swahili Verbs Work

In English, we change the whole verb: "I speak" → "I spoke" → "I will speak."

In Swahili, we keep the verb stem the same and just swap a small tense marker inside the verb. Think of it like a slot machine — one slot changes, the rest stays the same.

SUBJECT PREFIX + TENSE MARKER + VERB STEM

For example, the verb -sema (to speak) becomes:
Ni + -na- + sema = Ninasema (I am speaking)

-LI-

Remote Past Tense

Use this tense for things that happened a long time ago — yesterday, last week, last year, or in the past in general.

Think of it as the English "I did / I ate / I went."

Subject Prefix + -li- + Verb Stem
Whenever you say something happened before today, use -li-.
SwahiliBreakdownEnglish
Nilikula Ni + li + kula I ate
Ulienda U + li + enda You went
Alisoma A + li + soma He/She read
Tulilala Tu + li + lala We slept
Walimcheza Wa + li + cheza They played

-ME-

Recent Past / Perfect Tense

Use -me- when something just happened or happened recently, and the result is still felt now. This is like the English "I have eaten / She has arrived."

Key idea: the action is done, but it still matters right now.

Subject Prefix + -me- + Verb Stem
The difference between -li- and -me-: "Nilikula" = I ate (long ago, done). "Nimekula" = I have eaten (I'm full now!).
SwahiliBreakdownEnglish
NimekulaNi + me + kulaI have eaten
AmefikaA + me + fikaHe/She has arrived
TumemalizaTu + me + malizaWe have finished
WameendaWa + me + endaThey have gone
UmeponaU + me + ponaYou have recovered

Which sentence uses -me- correctly?

-NA-

Present Continuous Tense

Use -na- for things happening right now at this moment. It's like the English "I am eating / She is running."

Subject Prefix + -na- + Verb Stem
If it's happening AS you speak, use -na-. "Ninasema" = I am speaking (right now, in this moment).
SwahiliBreakdownEnglish
NinasemaNi + na + semaI am speaking
UnakulaU + na + kulaYou are eating
AnakimbiaA + na + kimbiaHe/She is running
TunachezaTu + na + chezaWe are playing
WanaimbaWa + na + imbaThey are singing

-HU-

Habitual Tense

The -hu- tense is special — it talks about things that happen regularly, always, or as a habit. It's like the English "I always eat rice" or "One usually speaks softly."

Important: The -hu- tense does NOT change with the subject. You don't say "Nihu-" or "Uhu-" — you just use hu- directly before the verb stem for all persons.

hu- + Verb Stem (no subject prefix needed)
Think of -hu- as saying "one always does this" or "it is customary to…" It describes routines and universal truths.
SwahiliBreakdownEnglish
Husemahu + semaOne usually speaks / (They) always speak
Hukulahu + kulaOne usually eats
Hulala mapemahu + lala + mapemaOne always sleeps early
Husoma asubuhihu + soma + asubuhiOne usually reads in the morning

🧠 Quick Check — Habitual (-hu-)

-TA-

Future Tense

Use -ta- for things that will happen in the future — tomorrow, next week, or any time ahead. It's like English "I will eat / She will go."

Subject Prefix + -ta- + Verb Stem
For a very distant future, some speakers use -taka- instead of -ta-, but -ta- works perfectly for everyday speech.
SwahiliBreakdownEnglish
NitakulaNi + ta + kulaI will eat
UtaendaU + ta + endaYou will go
AtasemaA + ta + semaHe/She will speak
TutasomaTu + ta + somaWe will read/study
WataimbaWa + ta + imbaThey will sing

🧠 Quick Check — Future Tense (-ta-)

AHe/She spoke

-NGE-

Conditional Tense

Use -nge- for "would" situations — hypothetical or imagined actions. Like English "I would eat / If she came, I would be happy."

For past hypotheticals ("would have"), use -ngeli-.

Subject Prefix + -nge- + Verb Stem
"Ningekula" = I would eat (if something were possible). Often used with the word kama (if): Kama ningekuwa na pesa, ningenunua gari. (If I had money, I would buy a car.)
SwahiliBreakdownEnglish
NingekulaNi + nge + kulaI would eat
UngesemaU + nge + semaYou would speak
AngeendaA + nge + endaHe/She would go
TungelalaTu + nge + lalaWe would sleep
WangelipaWa + nge + lipaThey would pay
