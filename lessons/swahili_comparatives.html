Grammar · Week 10

Comparatives & Superlatives

Kulinganisha — comparing two things and expressing the maximum degree of a quality

What are comparatives and superlatives?

A comparative is used to compare two nouns — showing they are equal, or that one is better/worse than the other. In English: "as tall as," "taller than," "less than." Swahili does all of this using specific words and verbs.

A superlative shows that something has the maximum degree of a quality compared to everything else. In English: "the tallest," "the best," "the worst." In Swahili, superlatives are formed by combining comparative words with -ote (all) or -o-ote (any).

There are two categories of comparatives — equality (things are the same) and superiority/inferiority (one is more or less than the other) — plus superlatives.

Overview
Three categories at a glance
Every comparison in Swahili falls into one of these three types.
Section A

Equality

Two things are the same or equal in some quality — "as ... as," "the same as," "resembles."

Section B

Superiority

One thing is greater, better, or more than another — "... than," "better than," "more than."

Section B

Inferiority

One thing is lesser, worse, or less than another — "less than," "become less."

Section C

Superlatives

Something is the most or least of all — formed by adding -ote (all) or -o-ote (any).

Section A
Comparatives of Equality
Used to show two things are equal or alike in some quality. Several words and verbs can be used, sometimes with or without kama.
Word / structure Meaning Swahili example English translation
kama as ... as / like
Kijana huyu ni shujaa kama mwanajeshi.
This youth is as brave as a soldier.
vilevile also / equally
Sisi tunasoma Kiswahili vilevile.
We study Swahili also.
sawa equal (in)
Mazulia yale ni sawa kwa sifa.
Those carpets are equal in quality.
sawasawa same / identical amount
Televisheni na kompyuta zinatumia kiasi sawasawa cha umeme.
A television and a computer use the same amount of electricity.
sawa na equal to / the same as
Miwani yangu ya jua ni sawa na yako.
My sunglasses are the same as yours.
lingana (na) match / harmonize with
Urefu wako unalingana na urefu wa mpenzi wako.
Your height matches your girlfriend's height.
fanana (na) resemble / look like
Sura za mapacha hawa zinafanana.
The faces of these twins resemble each other.
Tip: kama, vilevile, sawa, and sawasawa can all be used with or without each other. You can say kama alone or pair it with any of the others for more emphasis — e.g. sawa kama (equal as) or vilevile kama (also as).
Section B
Comparatives of Superiority & Inferiority
Used to show one thing is more or less than another. Superiority = one thing is greater. Inferiority = one thing is lesser.
Word / structure Meaning Swahili example English translation
kuliko ... than (superiority or inferiority)
Mtoto wangu ni hodari kuliko mtoto wako.
My child is cleverer than your child.
bora kuliko better than
Afya ni bora kuliko mali.
Health is better than wealth.
heri ... kuliko better to ... than
(starts the sentence)
Heri kununua nyumba kuliko kununua gari.
Better to buy a house than to buy a car.
afadhali ... kuliko better to ... than
(starts the sentence)
Afadhali kuchelewa kuliko kukosa.
Better late than to miss it.
zaidi ya more than
Mfuko wangu ni mpana zaidi ya mfuko wake.
My bag is wider than his/her bag.
zidi exceed / increase more than
Bei ya chakula imezidi mfumko.
The price of food has increased more than inflation.
shinda beat / be better than
Timu ya mpira ya Hispania imeshinda timu ya Uholanzi.
The Spanish soccer team is better than the Dutch team.
pita exceed / surpass
Unafahamu Kirusi kupita Kiingereza.
You understand Russian better than English.
duni ya less than (inferiority)
Masanduku ya hati yana uzani duni ya sanduku la betri.
The boxes of documents weigh less than the box of batteries.
pungua become less / reduce
Chakula kimepungua utamu.
The food has become less tasty.
Noun A na Noun B,
Noun B ni ...		 naming two, then comparing
Mohammad na Henry, Henry ni mwembamba.
Huyu na yule, yule ni mwembamba.
Mohammad and Henry, Henry is thinner.
This one and that one, that one is thinner.
Note on kuliko: kuliko can also work as a locative — meaning "where something exists" — not only as a comparative. Example: Hapa ni mahali kuliko na watu wengi sana — This is the place where there are many people.
heri and afadhali: Both mean "better" and must always appear at the start of the sentence. They also always need kuliko somewhere in the sentence to complete the comparison.
Section C
Superlatives
To say something is the best, worst, most, or least of all — combine any comparative word with -ote (all) or -o-ote (any). The same verbs and adjectives from Section B are used here.
Structure Meaning Swahili example English translation
bora kuliko ... wote the best of all
Baba yangu ni bora kuliko baba wote.
My dad is the best (better than all dads).
zaidi ya ... yote more than all
Mfuko wangu ni mpana zaidi ya mifuko yote.
My bag is wider than all bags.
zaidi ya ... wo wote more than any
Mfuko wangu ni mpana zaidi ya mfuko wo wote.
My bag is wider than any bag.
zidi ... zote exceed all
Bei ya chakula imezidi bei zote.
The price of food has increased more than all prices.
duni ya ... yote less than all
Masanduku ya hati yana uzani duni ya masanduku yote.
The boxes of documents weigh less than all boxes.
pungua kuliko ... vyote least tasty of all
Chakula kimepungua utamu kuliko vyakula vyote.
The food has become less tasty than all foods.
shinda ... zote beat all / best of all
Timu ya mpira ya Hispania imeshinda timu zote.
The Spanish soccer team has beaten all teams.
shinda ... yo yote able to beat any
Timu ya mpira ya Hispania inaweza kushinda timu yo yote.
The Spanish soccer team is able to beat any team.
pita ... zote exceeds all others
Unafahamu Kirusi kupita lugha nyingine zote.
Your understanding of Russian exceeds all other languages.
kuliko watu wote /
mtu ye yote		 more than all / any person
Henry ni mwembamba kuliko watu wote.
Henry ni mwembamba kuliko mtu ye yote.
Henry is thinner than all people.
Henry is thinner than any person.
-ote vs -o-ote: Use -ote for "all" (wote, yote, zote, vyote) and -o-ote for "any" (wo wote, yo yote, zo zote, cho chote). Both form superlatives — but -o-ote is even stronger, meaning "more than any single one imaginable."
