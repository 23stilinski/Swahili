§ 1 — Foundations

How a Swahili Sentence Is Built

In English, the basic sentence order is Subject → Verb → Object (SVO). Swahili follows the same basic order, but with a big twist: all of this information is often packed inside a single verb word.

A Swahili verb word can include: who is doing the action, when it happened, who/what it was done to, and the action itself — all in one compact word.

SUBJECT PREFIX + TENSE MARKER + (OBJECT MARKER) + VERB STEM + (EXTENSION)
The parts in brackets ( ) are optional. A simple sentence only needs: Subject Prefix + Tense + Verb Stem.

Let's Break Down a Real Sentence

Take the sentence: "Ninapenda chakula" — I love food.

Ni na penda = I am loving / I love

Then chakula (food) is the object, placed after the verb — just like English!

Now compare these three sentences — notice only the tense marker changes:

01
Ninapenda chakula. I love food. (right now / generally)
Ni = I na = present penda = love chakula = food
02
Nilipenda chakula. I loved food. (in the past)
Ni = I li = past penda = love chakula = food
03
Nitapenda chakula. I will love food. (future)
Ni = I ta = future penda = love chakula = food
The verb stem never changes. Only the prefix slot before it changes. This makes Swahili very systematic once you know the rules!

English vs. Swahili — Side by Side

English Swahili Notes
I eat rice. Ninakula wali. Subject merged into verb; object (wali) follows
She reads a book. Anasoma kitabu. A = she/he; na = present; soma = read
They played football. Walicheza mpira. Wa = they; li = past; cheza = play
We will sing a song. Tutaimba wimbo. Tu = we; ta = future; imba = sing
You have arrived. Umefika. No object needed; whole sentence is one word!

§ 2 — Going Deeper

Object Markers — Talking About Someone

So far, we've placed the object (the thing/person receiving the action) after the verb. But Swahili also lets you embed the object inside the verb using an object marker.

This is like saying "I love-him" instead of "I love him."

PersonObject MarkerExampleMeaning
me-ni-AnanipendaHe/She loves me
you-ku-NinakupendaI love you
him/her-m- / -mw-NinampendaI love him/her
us-tu-AnatusaidiaHe/She helps us
you (plural)-wa-NinawaambiaI tell you all
them-wa-NinawasaidiaI help them
Both "you (plural)" and "them" use -wa-. Context and the subject prefix tell you which one is meant.
01
Ninakupenda. I love you. (the famous phrase!)
Ni = I na = present ku = you (object) penda = love
02
Ananisaidia. He/She is helping me.
A = he/she na = present ni = me (object) saidia = help
03
Tutawasaidia. We will help them.
Tu = we ta = future wa = them (object) saidia = help

§ 3 — Asking Questions

Question Words — Nini, Nani, Wapi?

Asking questions in Swahili is simpler than you might think. You often just add a question word to a normal sentence — either at the beginning or the end. There's no need to rearrange the sentence like in English ("Do you...?" "Are they...?").

Yes/No questions are formed simply by adding Je, at the start or raising your voice at the end.

Question WordSwahiliExample SentenceMeaning
What? Nini? Hii ni nini? / Unafanya nini? What is this? / What are you doing?
Who? Nani? Huyu ni nani? / Nani anakuja? Who is this? / Who is coming?
Where? Wapi? Uko wapi? / Nyumba iko wapi? Where are you? / Where is the house?
When? Lini? Utakuja lini? / Alizaliwa lini? When will you come? / When was he/she born?
Why? Kwa nini? Kwa nini unalia? Why are you crying?
How? Vipi? / Jinsi gani? Unafanyaje? / Hali yako vipi? How are you doing it? / How are you?
How many? Ngapi? Una watoto wangapi? How many children do you have?
Which? Gani? Unataka chakula gani? Which food do you want?
Yes/No? Je, ...? Je, unajua Kiswahili? Do you know Swahili?
Tip: In Swahili, question words like wapi, lini, nini usually go at the end of the sentence — the opposite of English! e.g. "Uko wapi?" = "You are where?" = "Where are you?"
Nini vs Nani: Nini asks about things. Nani asks about people. Never mix them up!
01
Unakula nini? What are you eating?
Unakula = you are eatingnini = what
02
Anakuja lini? When is he/she coming?
Anakuja = he/she is cominglini = when
03
Kwa nini unalala? Why are you sleeping?
Kwa nini = whyunalala = you are sleeping
04
Je, unapenda muziki? Do you like music?
Je = yes/no markerunapenda = you likemuziki = music

§ 5 — Putting It All Together

Building Complete Sentences

Now let's combine everything — subject, tense, object marker, verb stem, object noun, and location/time words — into full, rich sentences.

Swahili sentences can have extra information added using simple words for time (leo = today, kesho = tomorrow, jana = yesterday) and location (hapa = here, pale = there, nyumbani = at home).

Useful WordSwahiliUsed in a Sentence
todayleoNinakula leo. — I am eating today.
yesterdayjanaNilikwenda jana. — I went yesterday.
tomorrowkeshoAtakuja kesho. — He/She will come tomorrow.
nowsasaNinafanya kazi sasa. — I am working now.
herehapaKaa hapa. — Sit here.
therepale / hukoNyumba iko pale. — The house is there.
at homenyumbaniNiko nyumbani. — I am at home.
verysanaNinakupenda sana. — I love you very much.

Complex Sentence Examples

01
Mama alipika chakula jana nyumbani. Mum cooked food at home yesterday.
Mama = subject a+li+pika = she cooked (past) chakula = food (object) jana = yesterday nyumbani = at home
02
Je, utanisaidia kesho asubuhi? Will you help me tomorrow morning?
Je = question marker u+ta+ni+saidia = you will help me kesho = tomorrow asubuhi = morning
03
Watoto wanacheza mpira hapa sasa. The children are playing ball here now.
Watoto = children wa+na+cheza = they are playing mpira = ball hapa = here sasa = now
04
Ninajua kwamba anakupenda sana. I know that he/she loves you very much.
Ninajua = I know kwamba = that (connector) ana+ku+penda = he/she loves you sana = very much
